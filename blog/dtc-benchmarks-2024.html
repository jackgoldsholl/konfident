DTC Return Rate Benchmarks by Category: What's Normal and What's a Problem

May 15, 2025 · 8 min read · By the Konfident team
← Back to blog

One of the most common questions DTC founders ask when they see their return rate for the first time: "Is this bad?" A 15% return rate sounds alarming. An 8% return rate sounds fine. But whether either number is good or bad depends entirely on what category you're in.

This post compiles what's available in public research on category-level return rates, explains the caveats in how these numbers are measured, and gives you a framework for interpreting your own numbers against available benchmarks.

Important caveat upfront: Granular, independently verified ecommerce-only return rate benchmarks by category are not widely available in public research. The NRF publishes overall retail return rate data; Appriss Retail's analysis for NRF provides some category context but not publicly at the SKU or ecommerce-only level. The ranges below are derived from multiple industry sources and represent our best synthesis of available data — not a single authoritative study. Use them as context, not gospel.

Overall ecommerce return rate benchmark

The NRF's most recent data (2024, conducted with Happy Returns) reports an overall retail return rate of 16.9% across all of US retail. This includes both physical retail and ecommerce combined — and ecommerce tends to run higher than brick-and-mortar because shoppers can't inspect items before buying. A reasonable working assumption for ecommerce-specific overall return rates is 15–25%, depending on the platform and category mix.

The numbers you should care more about than benchmarks

Benchmarks are useful for a single question: "Is our return rate in a range that's plausibly driven by industry norms, or is it an outlier that suggests a specific problem?" Beyond that, they're not particularly actionable.

The numbers that are more actionable:

Return rate by SKU

If your store's return rate is 15% but three SKUs are driving 60% of returns, the problem is specific and addressable. Store-wide average return rate masks this. Every DTC brand operating at meaningful scale should be tracking return rate by SKU at minimum, and ideally by variant (size, color, etc.).

Return reason by SKU

Most return management platforms (Loop Returns, AfterShip, Narvar, etc.) capture a reason code at return initiation. The reason code is imperfect — shoppers often select the least confrontational option rather than the true reason — but patterns in return reasons by SKU are diagnostic. High rates of "doesn't match description" on a specific product is actionable. High rates of "wrong size" on apparel SKUs is actionable in a different way.

Return rate vs. review signal clarity

An insight that emerges when you correlate return rate with review signal clarity: products where the reviews are specific and informative about fit, results, and use case tend to have lower return rates than products with vague or sparse reviews — even controlling for product quality. The information gap at point of purchase predicts the return gap after delivery.

What a "good" return rate looks like in your category

Rather than benchmarking against industry averages, a more useful frame is: what's achievable for a well-merchandised product in your category, with strong fit guidance and synthesized review signals?

For apparel, brands with detailed fit guidance and high review clarity can operate in the 12–18% range even in categories where the industry average is 20–25%. The improvement is attributable to pre-purchase information, not post-purchase friction.

For supplements, brands with honest results-timeline guidance (not "see results in 14 days" when reviews suggest 4–6 weeks) tend to see lower return and complaint rates than brands that set inflated expectations in their copy.

For home goods, brands with contextual photography and accurate dimension guidance outperform those without — because the "didn't match description" return driver is addressed upstream.
