The NRF's annual returns report is released every January and immediately quoted in a dozen DTC newsletters. The headline number — $890 billion in merchandise returned in 2024 — is staggering enough to share on LinkedIn. But the shareable number isn't the useful number.

This post breaks down what the 2024 NRF data actually tells you about your own store, what the numbers are measuring, and where the real insight lives for a DTC brand doing somewhere between $500K and $20M per year in revenue.

What the NRF is actually measuring

The NRF's "Consumer Returns in the Retail Industry" report, conducted in partnership with Happy Returns (a UPS company), surveys retailers across all of US retail — brick-and-mortar and ecommerce combined. The 16.9% figure is the average return rate across all retail in 2024, up from 8.1% in 2019.

A few things worth knowing about how this is measured:

  • The 16.9% figure includes both physical retail and ecommerce, and these return rates behave very differently. Physical retail return rates tend to be lower because shoppers can inspect items before buying. Ecommerce return rates are generally higher.
  • The report doesn't publicly break out a specific ecommerce-only return rate in its press releases. The 16.9% figure is retail-wide.
  • The $890B figure is based on the volume of returned merchandise in dollar terms, not a transaction count. A $1,200 returned sofa counts more than a $28 returned lip gloss.

The number that matters more: Return rates have more than doubled since 2019. This isn't a pandemic anomaly that corrected. It's a structural shift. The rise of free return policies, bracket buying (purchasing multiple sizes with intent to return), and the expectation gap that comes with online shopping have reset the baseline permanently.

What the return cause data tells you

The NRF report is accompanied by consumer survey data that breaks down why shoppers return. The most cited consumer survey on this topic is from DealNews, which found:

  • 65% — item didn't fit (size, sizing, dimensions)
  • 56% — product was damaged or defective
  • 44% — shopper was dissatisfied with the item
  • 31% — item didn't match the description or photos

Items 1, 3, and 4 — fit, dissatisfaction, and description mismatch — are all information failures. The shopper had an expectation. The product didn't meet it. The return happened because the purchase decision was made with insufficient information, not because the product was defective.

This is the distinction that most return prevention discussion misses. Logistics solutions (easier returns processing, better packaging) address returns after they happen. The upstream problem is a communication failure at point of purchase.

Category breakdowns: where the problem is worst

The NRF doesn't publish detailed category breakdowns in its public press releases, but industry sources (including Appriss Retail's historical analysis for NRF) have consistently shown these patterns:

  • Apparel and footwear: Consistently above-average, driven heavily by sizing and fit. Common estimates range from 20–30% for online apparel. Bracket buying (buying multiple sizes) is especially prevalent — 50% of Gen Z shoppers report doing this, per the NRF 2024 data.
  • Electronics: Typically 15–20%. Returns often driven by compatibility mismatches and setup complexity rather than quality issues.
  • Home goods and furniture: Lower overall (roughly 10–15%), but high dollar value per return. Returns often driven by color inaccuracy, size mismatches, or assembly frustration.
  • Beauty: Lower than average — typically 5–10% — partly because of hygiene concerns around reselling opened products, and partly because beauty shoppers tend to do more research before buying.
  • Supplements: Variable, but often lower because consumable products can't be restocked once opened.

The cost side of the equation

Returns don't just cost you the revenue from the initial sale. DealNews research, cited by Shopify, estimates that processing a return costs 20–65% of the item's original value when you factor in shipping, handling, inspection, restocking, and the loss in resale value for items that can't be resold as new.

For a $100 item returned by a customer, your true cost — including shipping both ways, labor, and diminished resale value — can run anywhere from $20 to $65. That's on a $100 item where your margin may already be under 40%.

At scale, the math gets brutal quickly. A brand doing $5M per year with a 20% return rate is processing $1M in returned merchandise annually. If average processing cost is 30% of item value, that's $300,000 per year in returns processing costs — before accounting for the lost margin on the original sales.

What the NRF data says about consumer psychology

The 2024 NRF data also includes consumer survey findings that are worth pulling out separately:

  • 76% of consumers say free returns are a key factor in deciding where to shop.
  • 59% research retailer return policies before holiday purchases.
  • 67% say they would avoid future purchases from a retailer after a negative return experience.

The free returns expectation has become a structural feature of the competitive landscape — you can't easily remove it without losing customers. But the third finding is particularly important: a bad return experience destroys LTV. The customer who returns a product and has a friction-filled experience is unlikely to buy from you again. The customer who returns a product easily and hassle-free has a higher probability of repurchasing — but you've still absorbed the cost of the original return.

The most durable solution to the return rate problem isn't making returns easier. It's reducing the number of returns that happen in the first place by closing the expectation gap before the purchase is made.

What this means for your store specifically

If you're a DTC brand on Shopify, the practical takeaways from the 2024 NRF data are:

  • Your return rate is probably above 10% even if you're in a "low-return" category. If you don't know your return rate by SKU, that's the first place to look.
  • The majority of your avoidable returns are driven by information failures at point of purchase — the shopper thought the product was something it wasn't.
  • Sizing/fit, expectations about texture/finish/results, and accuracy to photos or descriptions are the specific information failures driving most returns in most categories.
  • The return processing cost is almost certainly higher than you think once you fully load the labor, shipping, and restocking costs.
